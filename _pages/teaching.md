<h1>Teaching</h1>

<p class="stmt-lede">I teach at the intersection of <strong>computation and clinical application</strong>, where students arrive from very different backgrounds - computer scientists who have never seen a patient cohort, life scientists who have never written a model - and have to learn to work together.</p>

<section class="stmt-sec">
  <h2 class="psec-h">Teaching philosophy</h2>
  <p>My teaching is built around a single conviction: students learn advanced methods best when they apply them to <strong>real, messy, consequential problems</strong>, and when the path from "I understand this" to "I can build this" is made explicit. The same principle runs through my research - I want students to build systems they can <strong>explain</strong> and others can <strong>trust</strong>, not black boxes. I have supervised <strong>17+ Master's and doctoral students</strong> across AI, Statistics &amp; Data Science, Computer Science, and Biomedical Sciences, and I hold the <strong>Basic Teaching Qualification (BKO)</strong>, which grounds my practice in evidence-based pedagogy.</p>
  <ul class="stmt-agenda">
    <li><strong>Interdisciplinarity is a skill, not an accident.</strong> My courses deliberately mix computational and biomedical students and ask them to translate for one another. Learning to explain a federated-learning protocol to a clinician, or a clinical endpoint to an engineer, is part of the assessment, not a side effect.</li>
    <li><strong>From comprehension to construction.</strong> I scaffold each topic so students move from understanding a method to implementing and critiquing it. My open-source teaching materials (below) exist so that the gap between a lecture and a working prototype is as small as possible.</li>
    <li><strong>Real data, real stakes.</strong> Whether it is the Wisconsin Breast Cancer dataset in a federated-learning tutorial or live ECoG signals in a BCI demonstration, I anchor abstract methods in problems where correctness and interpretability matter.</li>
    <li><strong>Accessibility widens the pipeline.</strong> Years of outreach coaching - teaching programming to children and to women entering tech - have convinced me that who gets to learn advanced methods is a design choice. I make my materials approachable on purpose.</li>
  </ul>
</section>
